    Know Before You Invest

    Frequently Asked Questions

    A good ROI typically ranges between 8% to 15% annually. Experienced investors often target 15%+ total return by combining cash flow, property appreciation, tax benefits, and loan paydown. The right ROI depends on your risk tolerance, location, and investment strategy — a lower-risk market like the Midwest may yield 10% while a high-growth market like Austin may return more via appreciation.

    Negative pre-tax cash flow is common in high-demand cities and doesn't automatically make a deal bad. Investors still profit through three other channels:

    • Property appreciation — typically 3–5%/year on the full asset value
    • Tax benefits — depreciation and interest deductions can flip negative to positive after-tax
    • Loan paydown — tenants are effectively paying down your mortgage, building equity

    Use the Tax Benefits toggle in this calculator to see how after-tax returns change your picture.

    Real estate investors get access to three powerful deductions that most other investments don't offer:

    • Mortgage interest deduction — the interest portion of your loan payment is fully deductible
    • Property tax deduction — annual property taxes reduce your taxable rental income
    • Depreciation — the IRS lets you deduct 1/27.5th of the structure's value each year as a non-cash expense, even if the property is appreciating

    Combined, these can generate $5,000–$15,000+ in annual tax savings on a typical rental property, effectively subsidizing negative cash flow.

    The 1% rule states that monthly rent should equal at least 1% of the purchase price. Example: a $200,000 property should rent for $2,000/month.

    It's a useful quick-filter, but don't rely on it alone. High-appreciation markets (NYC, Austin, LA) rarely meet the 1% rule yet still deliver strong total returns. Use it to screen deals, then run the full analysis here to confirm.

    Most US lenders require 20% to 25% down for investment properties — higher than the 3–5% available for primary residences. Key points:

    • 20% down = standard conventional loan, best rates
    • 25% down = often required for 2–4 unit properties
    • Less than 20% = possible via portfolio loans or DSCR loans, but at higher rates

    A larger down payment improves cash flow but reduces your cash-on-cash return by deploying more capital. Use the Down Payment slider to find your optimal balance.

    The most common and costly mistakes are:

    • Overestimating rent — always verify with current comparable listings, not wishful thinking
    • Underestimating expenses — vacancy, maintenance, and capex surprises kill deals
    • Ignoring total return — focusing only on monthly cash flow misses 60–70% of what makes real estate profitable
    • Buying on emotion — a beautiful property in a weak rental market is still a weak deal
    • Skipping due diligence — always inspect, verify rent rolls, and review local tax rates before closing
